Welcome

My name is Kyle Paterson. Thanks for stopping by- it’s nice to meet you! I’d like to take you on a little journey through my personal story and travels.

My Upbringing

As a little boy, I remember listening to my mother recount her travels through Europe at the dinner table. Her stories from Italy, Finland, and Spain left me in awe as a kid. I would listen to every detail as she described the food, the trains, her host families, and her friends. As a kid this left me with a strong desire to cross borders and learn about foreign countries and cultures. I still remember my first time leaving the country in 2013. Even as a ten-year-old, I knew that experience would set a course for the rest of my life. Since then, I have had the opportunity to backpack through nine more countries, each trip shaping my outlook in meaningful ways.

WWOOF

In the summer of 2024, I had the opportunity to WWOOF (agricultural work exchange) in Dogliani, Italy. I stayed outside of a small agricultural town in a villa perched above the French Riviera. I had the opportunity to work for a wonderful Swiss couple, Freddy and Ursula. They quickly immersed me into their way of life and I learned about permaculture, alpine culture, and the value of natural living. My days were filled with gardening, milking goats, and harvesting produce. One of my projects was building a stone retaining wall and repairing centuries-old masonry. It’s meaningful to know that a piece of my effort still stands in those Italian mountains. I am forever grateful for the wonderful conversations I got to share over the dinner table with Freddy and Ursula and will forever hold that summer dear in my heart.

"Self-Agency"

Experiences like these have shaped how I approach problem-solving and creativity. As a solo traveler, you sometimes have to solve unfamiliar problems within a short amount of time. You might be dropped in the middle of a foreign country, not know anyone, and have no idea where the train is taking you. Yet, by some unplanned intuition I like to call "traveler’s legs," you always figure it out. I remember waking up early one morning in Lisbon with a big day of traveling planned. As always, I had meticulously planned our route the night before, documenting every leg of the journey in my notes app. It wasn't until I was looking at my notes on the metro that I realized we were on the right line but heading in the exact wrong direction from the transport station we needed to be at in 30 minutes. The traveler's legs took over instantly, and I whisked my friend out of the station the moment the doors opened—a moment when being a 400m athlete really helped. Without a second thought, I immediately signaled to a taxi and communicated our situation in my rudimentary Spanish and Portuguese. Luckily, an enthusiastic taxi driver named Jose came right to our aid. I quickly told him where we needed to go and how much time we had, and Jose got to work. He masterfully zipped through roundabouts, utilized unknown side roads, and predicted traffic patterns. He dropped us off with just a minute to spare. As I boarded our departing bus, and the doors closed behind us, I was left with an enduring feeling of self-agency. This might seem like a small situation, but any traveler knows how much missing your transport to another country can throw a wrench in your plans. You don't have time to overthink or be scared. At that moment, I understood you don't just wait for opportunities to appear—you create them. It was a powerful lesson in making my own luck.

My Friends

Travel, for me, has never been just about the views. It is about the people I meet along the way. The friendships I’ve formed and the stories I’ve shared with others on the road give every journey its deepest meaning. These connections are the reason I continue to travel, and they inspire me to keep seeking out new places and new people. I am a true extrovert and believe my relationships give meaning and a framework to life, helping me understand myself and, more importantly, the world we live in. I approach every new person as a friend I haven't met yet.

Track and Field

Track and field was my world for two years as a Division 1 athlete at URI. It was my outlet, a place to push myself to the absolute limit and see the results follow. I earned All-State, All-New England, and even All-American honors, and I cherish the memories and friendships I made on the running oval. One moment I remember vividly is breaking our school's 4x100 relay record with my friend Tristen (pictured above). We ran a 40.64, a time that earned us second place at our conference championships. To me, the race wasn't perfect, but they rarely are. When you spend weeks, months, and years drilling baton exchanges and perfecting your sprinting form, you start to notice the smallest details. Even a single mistimed step on your takeoff is noticeable, and it will stick with you. Track taught me a lot about myself and my pursuit of perfection. My coach always said that the fastest sprinters aren't the ones who can contract their muscles the fastest, but the ones who can relax them the fastest. It’s about getting out of your own way. Knowing this, I’ve learned that speed isn’t always a matter of strength. Sometimes, it’s the art of letting go—of fear, of tension, of all that holds you back. Though nagging injuries have since prevented me from returning to my best times, this experience has instilled in me a holistic and longevity-centered approach to my physical training, my friendships, and my life.

My journey so far has been a blend of study, work, and travel, each experience teaching me something new about resilience, curiosity, and connection. I believe that success isn’t just about personal achievement, but about what we share with others along the way.
